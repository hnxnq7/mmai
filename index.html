About Me

Hi, I'm Rachel! I'm a first-year at MIT studying Computer Science & Mathematics. I'm interested in building AI systems that can carry out powerful work in ways that are reliable and well-understood. I'm currently especially interested in how we evaluate and trust these systems — how do we know they're doing the right thing, and how can we make their behavior more predictable?

As AI systems move toward generating code and executing tasks from high-level specifications, I'm curious about how we can bridge the gap between intent and execution — introducing stronger forms of verification so that outputs actually match what we mean. Ideally, this would let people focus more on defining ideas and goals, while safely offloading the implementation to agents. Recently I've been exploring ideas around agents, training and evaluation pipelines, and how concepts from formal verification might help us reason about model behavior! :)

Final Project

Vision vs. Protocol Effects in EEG2Video:
Baseline Audit and Temporal Alignment on SEED-DV

with Emma Wang and Winston Qian  ·  Team Repo  ·  Project README

EEG-to-video decoding benchmarks like SEED-DV risk confounding genuine visual perception with protocol-induced cognitive artifacts. We audit the EEG2Video framework for run-position artifacts and data leakage, then propose chunk-level latency-aware contrastive alignment to enforce genuine dynamic tracking.

Final Results — LATA: Latency-Aware Temporal Alignment

Standard EEG–video alignment assumes that time t in EEG corresponds to time t in video — physically wrong, since neural responses always lag the stimulus by a biological delay δ (P100 ~100ms, P300 ~300ms). LATA learns this delay end-to-end with no supervision on δ, via a trainable softmax distribution over candidate delays and a latency-aware InfoNCE loss.
